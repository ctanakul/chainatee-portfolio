MOBILE BASE ROBOT FOR AIRPLANE MANUFACTURING

 

 

Background

At present, airplane parts are handled by humans. There has been an attempt to use robots to facilitate and substitute humans in some tasks. The plan for designing robots for airplane assembly has been initiated through the collaboration between Boeing and NxR Lab.

The robots are expected to work in a group of three. Each of them should be able to move freely in two dimensions on the factory floor while attached with an end-effector for grasping parts. A part will be held by the manipulator located on each robot. A human user must be able to control the movement of the part freely in six degrees-of-freedom.

This project is separated into two subtasks: the design and fabrication of the attached robot manipulator and the wheeled mobile robot. The responsility of our team is the latter subtasks. Therefore, this page documents only the development of the wheeled mobile robot.

This report constitutes three parts which are, the mobile base selection, the design and assembly of the mechanical system, and the electrical system of the mobile base.

The Mobile Base Selection

The mobile base should follow these baseline criteria:

  • The robots must be able to move freely in two dimensions on the factory floor.
  • While working in a group of three, the orientation of the handled part must be stationary.
  • A robot should support at least 35 kg.
  • It is preferred to use a 24V power supply that can be extended to other add-ons.
  • A robot can communicate with the primary computer system and other robots.
  • A robot can process self-odometry and operate based on the six degrees-of-freedom controlled by a human.

    • After several design reviews, the solution that met most of these criteria is Programmable Mecanum Wheel Vectoring Mobile Robot - IG 52 DB model

    .

    The mechanical system design and assembly

    The assembly of the robot comprises three parts, the upper, middle and lower floor of the robot. The upper floor will locate the robot manipulator. The middle floor will locate batteries and electrical components. The lower floor is where the mechanical system is situated.

    The mechanical system is a chain-driven system with double bearing support on each wheel. Each wheel is driven by a different motor.

    Above is the electrical schematic diagram of the system. We use two 12 Volt batteries to provide 24 Volt power supply for the system. The voltage is then converted into 5 and 12 Volt for use with a PIC microchip and Nvidia JETSON board. The board is used as an onboard computer to communicate with the PIC microchip to control the movement of the robot. The 24 Volt power supply is also used for two motor controllers. Each motor controller is connected to a Kangaroo x2 motion controller used as a device for tuning feedback controller of each wheel.

    Tuning the Kangaroo and setting up for RC Control

    The Kangaroo is a device for auto-tuning the Sabertooth motor controller. We also connect the RC receiver to the Kangaroo for controlling the robot remotely. The setup of the control is such that each axis of the joystick controls an individual wheel. For example, the throttle control (up-down on the left joystick) controls the rear left wheel, while the rudder control (left-right on the left joystick) controls the front right wheel. The right joystick has a similar setup for the other wheels.

    Progress and next step

    At present, the robot can be controlled by a joystick. However, the control is counter-intuitive from the RC controller. Thus, the next step is to control the robot with a microcontroller with a Linux computer using the Nvidia JETSON onboard computer to communicate with the PIC microchip. The robot manipulator also needs to be set up on the robot.
